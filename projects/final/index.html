How Our Clothing Is Killing The Environment
By Daniel Zika

Most of us have a lot of clothing in our closets. But when we're done with them what happens, how pollutant are our clothes and why does the industry that sells them create a large portion of our planets total C02 emissions.

In the 1980's, the average American bought 12 pairs of new clothing per year. Since then, that number has skyrocketed to close to 68 new pieces a year, which contributes to over 10% of our planet’s total CO2 emissions. The impact of all these wasted materials creates 1/5th of the world’s industrial water pollution and we as shoppers may be the only way to change the tides.

The main cause of all this buying is a a sector of the retail industry known as Fast Fashion. This inludes many big name brands like Zara, H&M, Fashionova and so many more.

Over the last 20 years, these brands have changed the entire clothing industry. Reacting to trends with fast cheap clothing for the masses. This developed this into the only real profitable part of the retail indursy, forcing many older name brands like GAP and Old Navy to adopt this new style of retail.

Cotton products are some of the most popular all over the globe but almost 90% of all cotton is produced with high amounts of toxic chemicals, that are known to spill into many of our precious natural resources all over the world. On top of that, popular athletic-wear brands, like Lululemon, create their synthetic materials out of crude oil, the aquisition of which is one of the most naturally destructive processes known to mankind.

In other sectors of the clothing industry there are brands like Patagonia and The North Face who have become staples for championing regenerative ways to manufacture clothing. Brands like this do their best to support organic farmers in the expensive process of getting verified as a real environemtally supportive source of cotton. This is extremely expensive for these brands, which in turn, pushes their prices up. Brands like this also tend to choose quality over quantity in their production.

In recent years, fast fashion brands have tried to change their images by pushing recycling programs that build their image, but much of this is just marketing to get customers back in the door with a small discount.
Even donating clothing doesn't solve the problem. Over 90% of all donated clothing still ends up being sold to textile retailers in developing countries where most clothing ends up in landfills.
The only real solution is buying less clothing, from more reputable companies, and wearing them a little bit longer.
Studies show that if Americans wore their clothing for just 9 more months and bought 1 used item instead of new, each of us would save around 6 tons of CO2 emissions each year.
That’s the equivalent of not driving for about a year and a half. All it takes is buying consciously, wearing longer, and occasionally looking for a used or fully recycled material item instead of a new one.
